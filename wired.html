What is a Wired Network?

A wired network is a system where devices are physically connected via cables — like veins channeling lifeblood between machines. These networks are renowned for their reliability, speed, and resistance to external interference.

Data travels through Ethernet cables (often twisted pair or fiber optics), connecting computers, servers, and printers to a centralized switch or router. It's the traditional method — old-school, grounded, and powerful.

Hardware Needed

To set up a wired network, you'll need specific components — the ritual tools of the networking underworld:

  • Router: Connects the local network to the internet — the gatekeeper.
  • Switch: Directs traffic within the network like a crypt warden.
  • Ethernet Cables: Typically Cat5e or Cat6 — the lifelines of data.
  • Network Interface Card (NIC): Installed in each device, allowing it to connect physically.
  • Patch Panel (optional): Keeps things organized in large setups — your cabling altar.

Pros & Cons of Wired Networking

All power comes at a cost. Wired networking offers many strengths but also bears its burdens:

🖤 Pros:

  • Speed: Wired networks typically offer faster data transfer rates than wireless (up to 10 Gbps or more).
  • Stability: No interference from walls, microwaves, or other ghosts in the air.
  • Security: Physical connections are harder to intercept than wireless signals — good for keeping secrets buried.
  • Low Latency: Perfect for gaming, streaming, and time-critical tasks like online necromancy.

💀 Cons:

  • Limited Mobility: Devices are tethered — a chain that binds you to one place.
  • Complex Setup: Requires planning, routing cables, and sometimes drilling through castle walls.
  • Clutter: Too many wires can summon the Cable Demon (or at least a messy workspace).
  • Cost: Especially in large buildings — installing cables can drain your coin pouch.
